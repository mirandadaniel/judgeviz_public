LIMITATION OF ACTION — Land, recovery of — Possessory title — Farmer remaining in occupation on determination of agreement — Land owner refusing requests for renewal — Whether occupiers' continuation of existing limited use evidencing intention to possess — Whether extinction of title by adverse possession amounting to deprivation of possession — Limitation Act 1980, s 15(1), Sch 1, paras 1, 8(1) — Human Rights Act 1998, s 3, Sch 1, Pt II, art 1

J A Pye (Oxford) Ltd and another v Graham and another [2002] UKHL 30

HL: Lord Bingham of Cornhill, Lord Mackay of Clashfern, Lord Browne-Wilkinson, Lord Hope of Craighead and Lord Hutton: July 4
Now published in The Law Reports: [2003] 1 AC 419

A squatter who asserted possessory title had to establish an intention to possess, not to own, the relevant land and to exclude the paper title owner only so far as was reasonably possible; accordingly his willingness to pay for occupation of the land, if asked to do so, was not inconsistent with his being in possession in the meantime.
The House of Lords so held when allowing an appeal by the defendants, Caroline Graham, in her own capacity and with Charles Denton, as personal representatives of the late Michael Graham, from the Court of Appeal [2001] Ch 804, which had allowed an appeal by the claimants, J A Pye (Oxford) Ltd and J A Pye (Oxford) Land Ltd, from the decision of Neuberger J [20002] Ch 676, who had dismissed their claim, inter alia, for possession to 25 hectares of agricultural land at Henwick, Thatcham, Berkshire on the ground that the defendants had established possessory title to the land for the purposes of s 15(1) of and Schedule 1, paras 1, 8(1) to the 1980 Act.
In February 1983 the claimants in a written agreement permitted G to occupy the land for grazing; when the agreement expired in December he remained in occupation although required to vacate it. The claimants refused requests to renew the agreement, indicating that they wanted the land not to be grazed while they sought planning permission for development. In August 1984 with the claimants' permission they took a cut of hay for payment and thereafter, without permission, continued to use the land for grazing and other agricultural uses. The land, which adjoined their farmland, was at all times enclosed by hedges and was only accessible, save on foot, via a padlocked gate to which only they had the key.

LORD BROWNE-WILKINSON said that for the purposes of the 1980 Act the words "possession" and "dispossession" bore their ordinary meaning so that "possession", as in the law of trespass or conversion, connoted a sufficient degree of occupation or physical control coupled with an intention to possess and "dispossession" occurred where the squatter assumed "possession" as so understood; the old notions, predating 1833, that the squatter should be required to oust or exclude the paper owner as well as all others or to act inconsistently with his user or adversely towards him had no place in the 1980 Act and the phrase "adverse possession" in para 8(1) was directed not to the nature of the possession but to the capacity of the squatter. To establish factual possession the squatter had to show absence of the paper owner's consent, a single and exclusive possession and such acts as demonstrated that in the circumstances he had dealt with the land as an occupying owner might normally be expected to do and that no other person had done so. The requisite intention was, not to own, but to possess and on one's own behalf in one's own name to exclude the world at large, including the paper owner, so far as was reasonably possible. It was not therefore inconsistent for a squatter to be willing , if asked, to pay the paper owner and to be in possession in the meantime. On the claimants' concession that the 1998 Act did not have retrospective effect, the principle of interpretation that legislation should be construed consistently with the Convention did not apply since the language of the 1980 Act was not ambiguous. On the evidence as a whole and the facts found by the judge, G was in factual possession and from September 1984 onwards had used the land as his own in a way normally to be expected of the owner; the claimants had done nothing on the land and were effectively excluded from it throughout that period. G had accordingly manifestly intended to assert possession, possessory title had been established and the judge's order would be restored.
Lord Mackay agreed; Lord Bingham, Lord Hope and Lord Hutton delivered concurring opinions.
