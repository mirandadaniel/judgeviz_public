SOCIAL SECURITY — Scotland — Provision of benefits — Residential care with nursing —Decision to provide — Whether person's capital to be taken into account — Social Work (Scotland) Act 1968, ss 12(3A) (inserted by Community Care (Residential Accommodation) Act 1998, s 2), 13A (inserted by National Health Service and Community Care Act 1990, s 56)

Robertson v Fife Council

HL: Lord Slynn of Hadley, Lord Mackay of Clashfern, Lord Nicholls of Birkenhead, Lord Hope of Craighead and Lord Hobhouse of Woodborough: 25 July 2002

A local authority in Scotland was not entitled to take into account a person's capital when deciding whether to provide residential nursing care for that person under s 13A of the Social Work (Scotland) Act 1968.
The House of Lords so held in allowing an appeal by Mrs Mary Robertson from the order of the First Division of the Court of Session (the Lord President (Rodger) and Lord Bonomy; Lord Weir dissenting) 2001 SLT 708 refusing her reclaiming motion against the decision of the Lord Ordinary (Lady Cosgrove)2000 SLT 1226 dismissing her petition for judicial review of Fife Council's decision, having assessed her as needing residential care with nursing, to refuse to enter into a funding arrangement for her to receive such care by reason of its determination that she had notional capital, said to have arisen from her transfer of the ownership of her house to her children in 1995, in excess of £16,000.
S 12(1)(2) of the 1968 Act imposed a duty on local authorities in Scotland to promote social welfare and provide assistance, including residential accommodation, to relevant persons. By s 12(3A): "In determining for the purposes of this section whether to provide assistance by way of residential accommodation to a person, a local authority shall disregard so much of the person's capital as does not exceed [£16,000]."
S 13A imposed a duty on authorities to provide residential accommodation with nursing to persons in need of such accommodation.

LORD HOPE OF CRAIGHEAD said that Fife had argued that statutory authority for their decision was to be found in s 12(3A). However, the provision of residential accommodation with nursing under s 13A was a separate exercise from the provision of residential accommodation under s 12. The powers and duties described in the two sections were different. The duty to be performed under s 13A was not to be seen as a particular example of the general duties to be performed under s 12 but as a separate and distinct duty. S 12(3A) did not apply on a s 13A determination. Further, the direction in s 12(3A) to "disregard" capital under the limit in the determination to which s 12(3A) referred did not indicate that capital above the limit was to be brought into account at that stage.
LORD SLYNN OF HADLEY, LORD MACKAY OF CLASHFERN, LORD NICHOLLS OF BIRKENHEAD and LORD HOBHOUSE OF WOODBOROUGH agreed.
