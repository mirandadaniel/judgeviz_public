CRIME— Sentence — Confiscation order — Committal to Crown Court for sentence by magistrates' court — Offences committed before 1998 — - Whether Crown Court having jurisdiction to make confiscation order — Whether lacuna in the law — -Criminal Justice Act 1988, s 71

R v Pope

HL: Lord Nicholls of Birkenhead, Lord Browne-Wilkinson, Lord Hobhouse of Woodborough, Lord Millett and Lord Scott of Foscote: 20 June 2002

S 42 of the Powers of Criminal Courts Act 1973 was clear and unambiguous and it gave the Crown Court power to deal with an offender who had been convicted in the magistrates' court and committed for sentence to the Crown Court as if he had just been convicted on indictment in the Crown Court. Subsequent legislative amendments did not create any lacuna in the law so as to restrict the Crown Court's powers to make confiscation orders pursuant to s 71 of the Criminal Justice Act 1988 in respect of offenders who had been committed for sentence by the magistrates' court and whose offences had been committed before 30 September 1998.
The House of Lords so held allowing an appeal by the Crown appeal from a decision of the Court of Appeal (Criminal Division) on 5 April 2001, allowing an appeal by the defendant, Alan Pope, against a confiscation order in the sum of £127,000 imposed by the Crown Court at Peterborough (Judge Morell sitting with justices) on 26 May 2000 on the defendant's committal for sentence pursuant to s 38 of the Magistrates' Courts Act 1980 after he pleaded guilty at Huntingdon Magistrates' Court on 20 January 2000 to 17 offences, committed between 1993 and 1997, of procuring the execution of a valuable security by deception contrary to s 20(2) of the Theft Act 1968 .

LORD HOBHOUSE OF WOODBOROUGH said that the Court of Appeal had concluded that they were bound by R v Whellem [2000] 1 Cr App R (S) 200 to hold that the Crown Court had no jurisdiction to make the confiscation order. The question of statutory construction had arisen from the manner in which the 1988 Act and other relevant Acts had been amended from time to time. Having referred to the course of the amending legislation and to the parties' submissions his Lordship said that s 42 was clear and so was the function of the s 38 scheme of which it formed part. The purpose was to enable the question of guilt in a case triable either way to be settled in the magistrates' court where the defendant did not opt for trial on indictment and for the Crown Court to deal with sentence where it was necessary to ensure that the sentencing court had the appropriate sentencing powers to reflect the gravity of the case. S 42 used wholly appropriate words to achieve that result. The supposed lacuna in the powers of the Crown Court to make confiscation orders did not exist even for the short period between the introduction of the new subsection (1E) to s 71 by the Proceeds of Crime Act in 1995 and the introduction, by the Crime and Disorder Act, of subsection (9A) to s 71 which came into force on30 September 1998. R v Whellem was overruled.
Lord Nicholls of Birkenhead, Lord Browne-Wilkinson, Lord Millett and Lord Scott of Foscote agreed.
Appearances: Victor Temple QC and Kennedy Talbot ( Crown Prosecution Service) for the Crown; Jeffrey Pegden QC and Geoffrey Birch (Brignalls, Balderston & Warren, Stevenage, Hertfordshire) for the defendant.
