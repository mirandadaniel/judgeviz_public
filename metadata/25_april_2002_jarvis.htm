LIMITATION OF ACTION — Breach of duty — Deliberate concealment of facts — Whether extending to negligent breach of which actor unaware — Limitation Act 1980, s 32(1)(b)(2)

Cave v Robinson Jarvis & Rolf (a firm) [2002] UKHL 18

HL: Lord Slynn of Hadley, Lord Mackay of Clashfern, Lord Hobhouse of Woodborough, Lord Millett and Lord Scott of Foscote: 25 April 2002
 Now published in The Law Reports: [2003] 1 AC 384

"Deliberate concealment" in s 32(1)(b) and (2) of the Limitation Act 1980 did not include failure to disclose a breach of duty that the actor was not aware of committing.
The House of Lords, overruling Brocklesby v Armitage & Guest (Note) [2002] 1 WLR 598, so held in allowing an appeal by the defendants, Robinson Jarvis & Rolf, from the Court of Appeal [2002] 1 WLR 581. The Court of Appeal, holding themselves bound by the decision in the Brocklesby case, had dismissed the defendants' appeal from Newman J, who had held that an action against them by the claimant, Martin William Cave, was not statute-barred.

LORD SLYNN OF HADLEY agreed with Lord Scott of Foscote.
LORD MACKAY OF CLASHFERN and LORD HOBHOUSE OF WOODBOROUGH agreed with Lord Millett and Lord Scott of Foscote.

LORD MILLETT said that s 32 deprived a defendant of a limitation defence in two situations: where he took active steps to conceal his own breach of duty after he had become aware of it, and where he was guilty of deliberate wrongdoing and concealed or failed to disclose it in circumstances where it was unlikely to be discovered for some time. It did not do so in a case of negligence if, being unaware of his failure to take proper care, there had been nothing for him to disclose and neither his original wrongdoing nor his failure to disclose it had been deliberate.

LORD SCOTT OF FOSCOTE said that the claimant had instructed the defendants to act for him in connection with a transaction securing him mooring rights for 100 years over a company's land. The transaction had been completed in March 1989. In February 1994 the company's receivers had told him that the rights were no longer exercisable. Letters by him to the defendants in 1995 and 1996 had been unanswered. On 16 January 1998 he had issued a writ against them for damages for negligence, complaining that the document drafted or approved by one C on behalf of the defendants had granted him merely contractual rights instead of rights enforceable against the company and its successors and that C had failed to protect the rights by registration. The defendants pleaded that the action was time-barred. In reply, the claimant alleged that the defendants' failure to answer his letters had constituted "deliberate concealment" within s 32(1)(b) of the 1980 Act and that their negligent drafting had been a deliberate commission of a breach of duty within s 32(2). It had been held in the Brocklesby case that s 32(2) came into play whenever there was an intentional act that constituted a breach of duty, whether or not to the knowledge of the actor. That was not correct. A breach of duty that the actor was not aware he was committing was excluded.

 
Appearances: Nicholas Davidson QC and David Drake (Beachcroft Wansbroughs, Bristol) for the defendants; Brian Doctor QC and Patrick Lawrence (Roach Pittis, Newport, Isle of Wight) for the claimant.
