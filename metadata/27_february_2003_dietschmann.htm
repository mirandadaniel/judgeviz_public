R v Dietschmann

Crime — Homicide — Diminished responsibility — Abnormality of mind resulting from alcohol and inherent causes — Direction to jury — Homicide Act 1957, 2(1)

R v Dietschmann

HL: Lord Nicholls of Birkenhead, Lord Lloyd of Berwick, Lord Hutton, Lord Hobhouse of Woodborough an Lord Rodger of Earlsferry: 27 Feb 2003
Now published in The Law Reports [2003] 1 AC 1209

A defendant seeking to prove a defence of diminished responsibility under s 2(1) of the Homicide Act 1957 in a case where he had taken drink prior to killing the victim, did not have to show that if he had not taken drink he would still have killed as in fact he did. He was entitled to the defence if the jury was satisfied that, despite the disinhibiting effect of the drink on the defendant's mind, his mental responsibility for his fatal acts was substantially impaired by an abnormality of mind arising from a cause specified in s 2(1).
The House of Lords so ruled in the appeal of the defendant, Anthony Dietschmann, from the dismissal by the Court of Appeal (Criminal Division) ( Rose LJ, Bell and Stanley Burnton JJ) on 5 October 2001, of his appeal from his conviction for murder before Maurice Kay J and a jury at Liverpool Crown Court on 7 April 2000.
The case was remitted to the Court of Appeal to decide whether to allow the appeal and quash the conviction for murder and order a new trial or to substitute a verdict of guilty of manslaughter for the verdict of murder found by the jury and to pass an appropriate sentence for that offence.

LORD HUTTON said that the defendant killed Nicholas Davies by punching him and kicking him in the head in a savage attack. At the time of the killing the defendant was heavily intoxicated and was also suffering from a mental abnormality . The judge, in summing up, told the jury that the defendant was entitled to the defence of diminished responsibility only if they were satisfied on the balance of probabilities that, (1) if the defendant had not taken drink he would still have killed and (2) he would have been under diminished responsibility when he did so. His Lordship said that s 2(1) meant that if the defendant satisfied the jury that, notwithstanding the alcohol he had consumed and its effect on him, his abnormality of mind substantially impaired his mental responsibility for his acts in doing the killing, the jury should find him not guilty of murder but, under s 2(3), guilty of manslaughter. That was because in referring to substantial impairment of mental responsibility s 2(1) did not require the abnormality of mind to be the sole cause of the defendant's acts in doing the killing. Even if the defendant would not have killed if he had not taken the drink, the causative effect of the drink did not necessarily prevent an abnormality of mind suffered by the defendant from substantially impairing his mental responsibility for his fatal acts.

LORD NICHOLLS OF BIRKENHEAD, LORD LLOYD OF BERWICK, LORD HOBHOUSE OF WOODBOROUGH and LORD RODGER OF EARLSFERRY agreed.
