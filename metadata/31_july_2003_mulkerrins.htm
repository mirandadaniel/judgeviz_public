Mulkerrins v Pricewaterhouse Coopers (a firm)

Bankruptcy — Property passing to trustee — Chose in action — County court order that trustee in bankruptcy had no interest in action by bankrupt against defendant — Whether defendant entitled to challenge order

Mulkerrins v Pricewaterhouse Coopers (a firm)

HL: Lord Bingham of Cornhill, Lord Nicholls of Birkenhead, Lord Millett, Lord Scott of Foscote and Lord Walker of Gestingthorpe: 31 July 2003

When a bankruptcy court ruled that a trustee in bankruptcy had no interest in a right of action in negligence which the bankrupt intended to bring, that decision could not be challenged by the defendants to the action in an application to strike out.
The House of Lords so held, allowing an appeal by the claimant, Barbara Anne Mulkerrins, from a decision of the Court of Appeal [2001] BPIR 106 allowing an appeal by the defendants, PricewaterhouseCoopers (formerly trading as Coopers & Lybrand), from a decision of Jules Sher QC sitting as a deputy judge of the High Court ([2000] BPIR 506), who had dismissed the defendants' application to strike out the claimant's action against the defendants brought following a declaration, granted by District Judge Henson in the Reading County Court sitting in bankruptcy on 3 February 1999, that the claimant's trustee in bankruptcy had no interest in the action.

LORD MILLETT, agreeing with Lord Walker of Gestingthorpe, said that the claimant sought damages from her former professional advisers, the defendants, for having negligently failed to protect her from bankruptcy. It did not matter whether the district judge's decision was right or wrong. Res judicata was a form of estoppel which gave effect to the policy of the law that parties to a judicial decision should not afterwards be allowed to re-litigate the same question, even though the decision might be wrong. If it was wrong, it must be challenged by appeal or not at all. The district judge's order therefore, bound the trustee and through him the creditors. As between the claimant and the creditors, her claim against the defendants and its proceeds belonged to her and did not form part of the bankrupt estate available to them. The defendants were not parties to the proceedings in the county court. They were not given notice of and took no part in them. They were not therefore bound by the district judge's order. But that did not mean that they could simply ignore it or that they were unaffected by it. It meant only that they could not be prejudiced by it. They could not re-litigate the issue, not because it was res judicata as against them, but because they had no legitimate interest in doing so. It would have been inappropriate to allow the defendants to take any part in the proceedings in the county court. Those proceedings were brought to resolve a dispute between the claimant on the one hand and her creditors, represented by the trustee on the other. The defendants were debtors with an adverse interest to the claimant and her estate. They could not be prejudiced by the vesting of the claim in her trustee; and a fortiori they could not be prejudiced if it remained in her. They had no legitimate interest in the question to be decided.
LORD WALKER OF GESTINGTHORPE agreeing, said that since the defendants had no right to be heard, it was irrelevant that it was not bound by the district judge's order in such a way as to create an estoppel per rem judicatam. Since the trustee in bankruptcy who was the only other possible contender for title to the right of action was bound by the order, its practical effect was not open to challenge by the defendants.

LORD BINGHAM OF CORNHILL and LORD SCOTT OF FOSCOTE agreed with LORD MILLETT and LORD WALKER. LORD NICHOLLS OF BIRKENHEAD agreed with LORD WALKER.
