Sepet and Another v Secretary of State for the Home Department

Immigration— Application for asylum — Turkish Kurds refusing to perform compulsory military service in Turkish army on conscientious grounds — Whether entitled to refugee status

Sepet and Another v Secretary of State for the Home Department

HL: Lord Bingham of Cornhill, Lord Steyn, Lord Hoffmann, Lord Hutton and Lord Rodger of Earlsferry: 20 March 2003
Now published in The Weekly Law Reports: [2003] 1 WLR 856

A conscientious objector to military service who was liable to imprisonment for refusing to serve if returned to his home country was not a refugee within the meaning of article 1A(2) of the Convention and Protocol relating to the Status of Refugees (1951) (Cmd 9171) and (1967) (Cmnd 3906).
The House of Lords so held in dismissing an appeal by the applicants, Yasin Sepet and Erdem Bulbul, from a decision of the Court of Appeal (Waller, Laws and Jonathan Parker LJJ) of 11 May 2001 dismissing their appeals from a decision of the Immigration Appeal Tribunal (Collins J and P R Moulden) dated 18 May 2000 which dismissed their appeals from separate decisions of the special adjudicator (J R L G Varcoe CMG) against the refusal by the Secretary of State for the Home Department to grant them asylum as refugees.

LORD BINGHAM OF CORNHILL said that there was compelling support for the view that refugee status should be accorded to one who had refused to undertake compulsory military service on the grounds that such service would or might require him to commit atrocities or participate in a conflict condemned by the international community, or where his refusal would earn grossly excessive or disproportionate punishment. But the applicants could not, on the facts as found, bring themselves within any of those categories. Nor had they been found to have a rooted objection to all military service, or an objection based on religious belief. Their unwillingness was based on strong and sincere opposition to the Turkish Government's policy towards their own Kurdish community. Their fear of the treatment which they would receive if returned to Turkey and maintained their refusal was, without doubt, well-founded. The crucial question was whether that treatment was to be regarded, for Convention purposes, as persecution for one or more Convention reasons. The core of their argument was that there existed a fundamental, internationally recognised, right, to refuse to undertake military service on grounds of conscience. The leading international human rights instruments, literally interpreted, gave little assistance to that argument. However, the reach of an international human rights Convention was not forever determined by the intentions of those who originally framed it. Thus the House was appropriately asked to consider a mass of material illustrating the movement of international opinion since the major relevant Conventions were adopted. From that it was plain that several respected human rights bodies had recommended and urged member states to recognise a right of conscientious objection to compulsory military service, to provide a non-combatant alternative and to consider the grant of asylum to genuine conscientious objectors. But such resolutions and recommendations could not themselves establish a legal rule binding in international law. It was hard for the applicants to show that there was clear international recognition of the right for which they contended, at any rate as of now. That temporal limitation was important, since international opinion was dynamic and the House could not do more than give effect to what it understood to be the current position. The applicants' contention did not find compelling support in the decided cases. The problem was that the treaties had treated compulsory military service as an exception from the forced labour prohibition without making any other provision, and there was not, as yet, a new consensus.
It had additionally been argued that, in deciding whether an asylum applicant had been or would be persecuted for Convention reasons, examination of the circumstances should be approached from the perspective of the persecutor, since that was the determinative perspective in inciting the persecution. Dyson LJ's statement in Sivakumar v Secretary of State for the Home Department ([2002] INLR 310, 317, para 23) that "It is necessary for the person who is considering the claim for asylum to assess carefully the real reason for the persecution" seemed to be a clear, simple and workmanlike test which gave effect to the 1951 Convention, provided that it was understood that the reason was the reason which operated in the mind of the persecutor and not that which the victim believed to be the reason for the persecution, and that there might be more than one real reason.

LORD HOFFMANN delivered a concurring opinion. LORD STEYN, LORD HUTTON and LORD RODGER OF EARLSFERRY agreed.
