P (A Minor) v National Association of School Masters/Union of Women Teachers [2003] UKHL 8

Trade dispute — Act in furtherance of — Ballot — Union failing to send ballot papers to two members entitled to vote — Ballot resulting in 26 to nil in favour of industrial action — Whether action having support of ballot — Action consisting of teachers' refusal to accept direction to teach disruptive pupil — Whether trade dispute — Trade Union and Labour Relations (Consolidation) Act 1992, ss 227(1),230(2), 232A, 232B (as inserted by Employment Relations Act 1999, Sch 3, paras 8, 9), 244(1)

P (A Minor) v National Association of School Masters/Union of Women Teachers [2003] UKHL 8

HL: Lord Bingham of Cornhill, Lord Hoffmann, Lord Hobhouse of Woodborough, Lord Scott of Foscote and Lord Walker of Gestingthorpe: 27 February 2003
Now published in The Weekly Law Reports [2003] 2 WLR 545

A dispute between a head teacher, who directed teaching staff to teach an allegedly disruptive pupil, and the staff, who refused to do so, was about their terms and conditions of employment so as to come within the meaning of a "trade dispute" in s 244(1) of the 1992 Act.
Where the union accidentally failed to send ballot papers to two members, whom it would be reasonable for it to believe would be induced to participate in the proposed action, the union did not fail to accord them entitlement to vote for the purposes of s 232A of the Act, and accordingly that omission did not invalidate the ballot.
The House of Lords so held when dismissing an appeal by P from the Court of Appeal [2001] ICR 1241 which had dismissed his appeal from Morison J, The Times, May 3, 2001. The judge had refused P's claim for an injunction to restrain the National Association of School Masters/Union of Women Teachers from action by which he was prevented from being taught in class.

LORD HOFFMANN said that the dispute was plainly over "terms and conditions", a composite expression which was intended to bear a broad meaning; it accordingly fell within the definition of a "trade dispute" in s 244(1). As to the validity of the ballot: s 227(1) defined the constituency who had to be accorded entitlement to vote so as to include all members whom it was reasonable for the union to believe would be induced to take part in the proposed action and no others. Compliance with the statutory provisions relating to the conduct of the ballot in respect of that constituency signified that its members had been accorded entitlement to vote. However ballot papers might, by s 230(2), be distributed "so far as is reasonably practicable". That showed that if it was not "reasonably practicable" the omission did not amount to a denial of entitlement. In any event s 232B provided for both ss 227(1) and 230(2) to be subject to the disregard of small accidental failures which were unlikely to affect the result. A failure to send a paper to a person within the constituency which fell within those exceptions did not deny his entitlement to vote. Accordingly the union's omission, which here was immaterial to the result, did not attract the negative condition in s 232A and the action had the protection of the ballot.

LORD BINGHAM and LORD WALKER delivered concurring opinions and LORD HOBHOUSE and LORD SCOTT agreed.
