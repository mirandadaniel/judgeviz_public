Shalson v Keepers and Governors of the Free Grammar School of John Lyon: UKHL 32

Landlord and tenant — Leasehold enfranchisement — Tenant's right to acquire reversion — Calculation of amount payable — Tenant's predecessor in title converting house into flats — Tenant's conversion back to single dwelling raising value of house — Whether conversion to single dwelling "improvement carried out by tenant" to be taken into account in assessing price payable — Leasehold Reform Act 1967, s 9(1A) (d) (as inserted by Housing Act 1974, s 118(4)

Shalson v Keepers and Governors of the Free Grammar School of John Lyon: UKHL 32

HL: Lord Bingham of Cornhill, Lord Steyn, Lord Hoffmann, Lord Millett and Lord Scott of Foscote: 12 June 2003

Work carried out by a tenant reconverting a house to a single dwelling from a conversion into flats by a previous tenant was an "improvement" which increased the value of the house at the valuation date when the tenant gave notice of his claim to acquire the freehold under the Leasehold Reform Act 1967; he was accordingly entitled, under s 9(1A) (d), to a diminution in the purchase price by the extent to which the house value was thereby increased.
The House of Lords so held when allowing an appeal by the tenant, Mr Peter Shalson, from the Court of Appeal [2003] Ch 110 dismissing his appeal from the Lands Tribunal (Mr N J Rose FRICS) whereby he uphold the decision of a Leasehold Valuation Tribunal that works by the tenant and certain of his predecessors at 98, Hamilton Terrace, London NW8 reconverting the house to a single dwelling from the flats to which it had been converted in 1947 fell to be disregarded in determining the price to be paid by the tenant in purchasing the freehold from the landlord, the Keepers and Governors of the Free Grammar School of John Lyon.

LORD HOFFMANN said that the language of s 9(1A)(d) was clear. A diminution in the open market value was to be allowed only by the extent to which that value had been increased by "any improvement" which had been carried out by the tenant or a predecessor at their own expense. To secure a reduction the tenant had first to identify such works and secondly satisfy the tribunal that but for them the house and premises would have been worth less."Improvement" in general meant additions or alterations which were not mere repairs or renewals: it was a physical not an economic concept, referring to the works themselves and not to the effect, if any, they had on the value of the premises. On the second condition: what did it mean to say that the value of the house and premises had been increased by the improvement ? It signified a simple causal relationship: but for the improvement, the house and premises would have been worth less. The comparison was between the value of the house as it stood and what its value would have been if the improvement had not been made. By that test the conversion works and the reconversion were both improvements but the former made no difference to the house value at the valuation date, because they had ceased to exist. On the other hand, if reconversion had not taken place, the 1947 improvements would still have existed and the house would have been worth less. To the extent that it was worth more, the tenant was entitled to a reduction in the open market value.
LORD BINGHAM OF CORNHILL and LORD MILLETT delivered concurring opinions; LORD STEYN agreed with LORD HOFFMANN, LORD BINGHAM and LORD MILLETT; and LORD SCOTT OF FOSCOTE agreed with LORD HOFFMANN.
