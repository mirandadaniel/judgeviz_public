In re Kanaris

Crime — Practice — Preparatory hearing — Whether permissible to hold separate hearings for co-defendants — Criminal Procedure and Investigations Act 1996, ss 29-31 — Prosecution of Offences (Custody Time Limits) Regulations 1987 (SI 1987/299), reg 5(6B) (inserted by the Prosecution of Offences (Custody Time Limits) (Amendment) Regulations 2000 (SI 2000/3284), reg 2)

In re Kanaris

HL: Lord Nicholls of Birkenhead, Lord Slynn of Hadley, Lord Steyn, Lord Hope of Craighead and Lord Hutton: 30 January 2003
Now published in The Weekly Law Reports [2003] 1 WLR 443

A judge in the Crown Court was allowed to conduct two separate preparatory hearings in respect of different defendants charged with criminal offences in the same indictment.
The House of Lords so held in allowing an appeal by the Crown from the order of Andrew Smith J, sitting in the Administrative Court of the Queen's Bench Division, granting a writ of habeas corpus ad subjiciendum to the respondent, Andreas Kanaris, one of 11 defendants awaiting trial on a single indictment.

LORD HUTTON said that under reg 5(6B) of the Prosecution of Offences (Custody Time Limits) Regulations 1987 the maximum period that the respondent could be held in custody before the start of his trial was 182 days. That period had been due to expire on 21 October 2001. On 7 September 2001 a hearing in the case of all 11 defendants took place before a judge in the Crown Court which the prosecution applied to be treated as a preparatory hearing under s 29 of the Criminal Procedure and Investigations Act 1996. S 30 of that Act provided that if a judge ordered a preparatory hearing the trial would start with that hearing. The judge began a preparatory hearing in respect of all the defendants except the respondent, whose counsel had raised an issue as to his fitness to plead, and one other defendant who was applying for the charges against him to be dismissed. Thereafter, on 5 October 2001, when the respondent's fitness to plead was no longer an issue, the judge held a preparatory hearing in respect of him. The judge in the Administrative Court had held, inter alia, that a preparatory hearing could not take place against only some defendants charged on an indictment and, since in his view there had been no valid preparatory hearing, the reg 5(6B) custody time limit period had expired.
In His Lordship's opinion the judge in the Administrative Court had erred. The law was clear that where a number of defendants were charged in the same indictment or in the same count, each defendant was charged with having himself committed an offence. There was no rule of statute or practice which prevented a judge from ordering a separate preparatory hearing in respect of one defendant jointly charged with other defendants in the same indictment if he considered that it was in the interests of justice to do so. The present case was one where the judge in the Crown Court had been fully entitled to take the view that it was desirable to make such an order.
His Lordship added that custody time limits were an important safeguard for accused persons and the effect of a decision to hold a preparatory hearing on an accused person's custody time limit had to be given careful consideration by the prosecution and judge.
LORD HOPE OF CRAIGHEAD delivered a concurrring speech and LORD NICHOLLS OF BIRKENHEAD, LORD SLYNN OF HADLEY and LORD STEYN agreed.
