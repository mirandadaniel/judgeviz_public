CONTEMPT OF COURT - Sources of information - Disclosure - Secure patient's medical records disclosed to newspaper - Whether disclosure of informant's identity necessary in "interests of justice" - Contempt of Court Act 1981 (c 49), s 10

Ashworth Hospital Authority v MGN Ltd

HL: Lord Slynn of Hadley, Lord Browne-Wilkinson, Lord Woolf, Lord Nolan and Lord Hobhouse of Woodborough: 27 June 2002

An order against the defendants for disclosure of the source of information originating in the claimant authority's medical records and used in a newspaper article had been rightly made.
The House of Lords so held in dismissing an appeal by the defendants, MGN Ltd, from the order of the Court of Appeal (Lord Phillips of Worth Matravers MR, May and Laws LJJ) [2001] 1 WLR 515 affirming Rougier J.

LORD SLYNN OF HADLEY delivered an opinion agreeing with Lord Woolf.
LORD BROWNE-WILKINSON agreed with Lord Woolf and Lord Hobhouse of Woodborough.
LORD WOOLF said that an article in the "Daily Mirror" had included verbatim extracts from the medical records of Ian Brady, one of the Moors murderers, a patient at Ashworth Security Hospital. The claimant authority, responsible for the management of the hospital, wished to discover the source of the leak of information, probably an employee at the hospital, and the judge had ordered the defendants to make and serve a witness statement to that end. Under the Norwich Pharmacal jurisdiction to order disclosure (Norwich Pharmacal Co v Customs and Excise Comrs [1974] AC 133) there was no requirement that the person against whom the proceedings were brought should himself be a wrongdoer: it was sufficient that he was involved in or participated in the wrongdoing of the person whose identity it was sought to establish. An order could be made although the information was required not for the purpose of bringing proceedings against the informant but with a view to his dismissal. The situation had to be exceptional to justify an order for disclosure of a journalist's sources, but disclosure of patients' records increased the difficulty and danger involved in care of patients at the authority's hospital and it was essential, to deter the same or similar wrongdoing in the future, that the source should be identified and punished. The fact that the patient himself had put similar material into the public domain did not detract from the need to prevent staff from revealing patients' medical records.
LORD NOLAN agreed with Lord Woolf and Lord Hobhouse of Woodborough.
LORD HOBHOUSE OF WOODBOROUGH delivered an opinion agreeing with Lord Woolf.
Appearances: Desmond Browne QC, Richard Parkes and Anna Coppola (Kerman & Co) for the defendants; Nigel Pleming QC and Vincent Nelson QC (Reid Minty) for the authority.
