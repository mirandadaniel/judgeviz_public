Bellinger v Bellinger

Husband and wife — Marriage — Validity — Wife born male but undergoing gender reassignment treatment — Whether marriage to a man valid — Whether non-recognition of gender reassignment violations of right to respect for private and family life and right to marry — Matrimonial Causes Act 1973, s 11(c) — Human Rights Act 1998 S 4, , Sch I, Pt I, arts 8 and 12

Bellinger v Bellinger

HL: Lord Nicholls of Birkenhead, Lord Hope of Craighead, Lord Hobhouse of Woodborough, Lord Scott of Foscote and Lord Rodger of Earlsferry: 10 April 2003
Now published in The Weekly Law Reports: [2003] 2 WLR 1174

A person who was born male but had undergone gender reassignment treatment could not enter into a valid marriage with another male because they were not "respectively male and female" within the meaning of s 11(c) of the Matrimonial Causes Act 1973. However, the non-recognition of gender reassignment for the purpose of enabling transsexual persons to marry was incompatible with the right to respect for private and family life and the right to marry guaranteed by arts 8 and 12 respectively of the European Convention for the Protection of Human Rights and Fundamental Freedoms (1953) (Cmd 8969).
The House of Lords so held on the appeal of the petitioner, Elizabeth Ann Bellinger, supported by the respondent, Michael Jeffrey Bellinger, from a decision of the Court of Appeal [2002] Fam 150 dismissing her appeal from a decision of Johnson J on 2 November 2000 refusing to grant a declaration that the petitioner's marriage to the respondent was valid at its inception and subsisting notwithstanding the fact that the petitioner was born a male.
The petitioner was correctly registered at birth as male, but had undergone gender reassignment surgery and treatment by the time she married the respondent in 1981.

LORD NICHOLLS OF BIRKENHEAD said that recognition of the petitioner as a female for the purposes of s 11(c) would necessitate giving the expressions "male" and "female" a novel extended meaning so that a person might be born with one sex, but might become, or become regarded as, a person of the opposite sex. That would represent a major change in the law, having far reaching ramifications. It raised issues whose solutions called for extensive inquiry and the widest public consultation and discussion. The questions of social policy and administrative feasibility which arose at several points, and their interaction had to be evaluated and balanced. The issues were altogether ill-suited for determination by courts and court procedures. A change in the law as sought by the petitioner must be a matter for deliberation and decision by Parliament . But it was clear that the non-recognition of gender reassignment for the purposes of marriage was compatible with arts 8 and 12 of the Convention. It was desirable that in a case of such sensitivity as this one, their Lordships as the court of final appeal in the country should formally record that the present state of statute law was incompatible with the Convention. A declaration would accordingly be granted that s 11(c) was incompatible with arts 8 and 12 of the Convention.

LORD HOPE OF CRAIGHEAD, LORD HOBHOUSE OF WOODBOROUGH and LORD RODGER OF EARLSFERRY delivered concurring speeches. LORD SCOTT OF FOSCOTE agreed with LORD NICHOLLS and LORD HOPE.
