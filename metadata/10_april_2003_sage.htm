Sage v Secretary of State for the Environment, Transport and the Regions and another

Town planning — Enforcement notice — Validity — Partial construction of dwelling house in breach of planning control — Whether notice served within four years of operations being "substantially completed" — Town and Country Planning Act 1990, ss 55(2)(a), 171B(1)

Sage v Secretary of State for the Environment, Transport and the Regions and another

HL: Lord Nicholls of Birkenhead, Lord Hope of Craighead, Lord Hobhouse of Woodborough, Lord Scott of Foscote and Lord Rodger of Earlsferry: 10 April 2003
Now published in The Weekly Law Reports: [2003] 1 WLR 983

The exception in s 55(2)(a) of the Town and Country Planning Act 1990 to "development" for which planning permission was required applied only to a completed building on which work was being carried out for its maintenance, improvement or other alteration and not to the work involved in completing an incomplete structure which was subject to planning control.
Therefore, even if the work remaining to be done on an incomplete dwelling house was only internal work which did not affect the external appearance of the building, the outstanding work was still subject to planning permission and the building could not be regarded as "substantially completed" for the purposes of s 171B(1).
The House of Lords so held, allowing Maidstone Borough Council's appeal from the dismissal by the Court of Appeal ( Schiemann, Keene LJJ and Sir Murray Stuart-Smith) on 28 June 2001 of their appeal from a decision of Mr Duncan Ousley QC sitting as a Deputy High Court Judge on 11 October 2000. The judge had allowed an appeal by Alan Frank Sage from the decision, dated December 16 1999, of an inspector appointed by the Secretary of State for the Environment, Transport and the Regions, upholding an enforcement notice served on 19 March1999 requiring Mr Sage to demolish a partially erected dwelling house on the ground that it was constructed in breach of planning control.


LORD HOBHOUSE OF WOODBOROUGH said that the appeal concerned the construction of s 171B(1) of the 1990 Act and the starting point of the four-year period within which an enforcement notice had to be served: namely, "the date on which the operations were substantially completed". Mr Sage challenged the notice on the ground that it was out of time since no work had been carried out on the structure in the four years before the notice was served. The inspector concluded that the building was a dwelling house "in course of construction". Mr Sage submitted that since all the work remaining to be done was either internal work or work which did not materially affect the external appearance of the building it came within the exception to "development" in s 55(2)(a) of the Act and therefore did not require planning permission. He argued that the enforcement notice could only relate to breaches of planning control and once no further breach was involved in completing the development there could be no further building operations to which an enforcement notice could apply and therefore the operations referred to in s 171B(1) must have been completed.
His Lordship said that the exception in s 55(2)(a) applied only to work carried out for the "maintenance, improvement or other alteration" of a completed building and not to work carried out by way of completing an incomplete structure. The work required to complete the incomplete dwelling house did not come within the exception and required planning consent. If a building operation was not carried out, both externally and internally, fully in accordance with the planning permission granted, the whole operation was unlawful. The notice had not been served outside the period of four years starting from the date on which the building operations were substantially completed.

LORD NICHOLLS OF BIRKENHEAD and LORD RODGER OF EARLSFERRY agreed.

LORD HOPE OF CRAIGHEAD and LORD SCOTT OF FOSCOTE delivered concurring speeches.
