LOCAL GOVERNMENT — Community care services — Discharged mental patients — After-care services — Whether authorities entitled to charge for residential accommodation — Mental Health Act 1983, s 117

R v Manchester City Council, Ex p Stennett
R v Redcar and Cleveland Borough Council, Ex p Armstrong
R v Harrow London Borough Council, Ex p Cobham

HL: Lord Slynn of Hadley, Lord Mackay of Clashfern, Lord Steyn, Lord Hutton and Lord Millett: 25 July 2002

Local social service authorities were not allowed to charge for after-care services provided pursuant to s 117 of the Mental Health Act 1983 to persons in their area who had been discharged from compulsory detention under s 3 of that Act.
The House of Lords so held in dismissing appeals by Manchester City Council, Redcar and Cleveland Borough Council and Harrow London Borough Council from the order of the Court of Appeal (Otton and Buxton LJJ and Hooper J) sub nom R v Richmond upon Thames London Borough Council, Ex p Watson [2001] QB 370 affirming the decision of Sullivan J [2000] LGR 318 to allow judicial review applications by four discharged patients to quash the decision of their authority to charge for accommodation provided under s 117. The fourth discharged patient, who had brought proceedings against Richmond upon Thames London Borough Council, died before the appeal to the House.
By s 117(2): "...It shall be the duty of the ...local social services authority to provide after-care services for any person [specified in s 117(1), including those discharged from a s 3 detention] until ... satisfied that the person concerned is no longer in need of such services ..."

LORD STEYN said that the three cases before the House all involved mentally ill persons who were formally admitted to hospital under s 3, discharged, and placed in caring residential accommodation by their authority pursuant to s 117 but charged for that accommodation. The central question was whether s 117 authorised and required the provision of the after-care services, in which case there was no right to charge because s 117 contained no charging provision, or whether it merely operated as a gateway section to trigger provisions under other statutory provisions, the relevant one in respect of residential accommodation being s 21 of the National Assistance Act 1948, which contained an express charging provision. His Lordship said that if Parliament had intended by s 117(2) that after-care services would be provided under other statutory provisions, one would have expected s 117(2) to specify the statutory provisions to be triggered. Such a gateway provision would require appropriate wording, as in s 2 of the Chronically Sick and Disabled Persons Act 1970. Instead s 117 was free-standing and itself imposed a duty on the authorities to provide the after-care services. It was incapable of being read as a duty to secure the provision of such services under unnamed other enactments. Counsel for the authorities had accepted in oral argument that there was a duty under s 117 to provide after-care services but had disputed that there was any power under the section to provide the services. However, the natural and obvious interpretation of s 117(2) was that the duty by necessary implication imported a concomitant power to carry out the duty.
LORD SLYNN, LORD MACKAY, LORD HUTTON and LORD MILLETT agreed.
