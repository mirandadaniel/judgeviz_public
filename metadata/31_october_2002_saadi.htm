R (Saadi and Others) v Secretary of State for the Home Department

Immigration — Application for asylum — Detention pending determination of application — Whether lawful — Immigration Act 1971, Sch 2, para 16(1) — Human Rights Act 1998, Sch 1, Pt I, art 5(1)(f)

R (Saadi and Others) v Secretary of State for the Home Department

HL: Lord Nicholls of Birkenhead, Lord Mustill, Lord Slynn of Hadley, Lord Hutton and Lord Scott of Foscote: 31 October 2002

The policy of detaining certain asylum seekers for a short period at a dedicated centre set up to ensure speedy and efficient processing of applications was lawful and did not infringe art 5(1)(f) of the Convention for the Protection of Human Rights and Fundamental Freedoms, as scheduled to the Human Rights Act 1998.
The House of Lords so held in dismissing appeals by four asylum claimants from the order of the Court of Appeal (Lord Phillips of Worth Matravers MR, Schiemann and Waller LJJ) [2002] 1 WLR 356 allowing an appeal by the Secretary of State for the Home Department from Collins J who had granted applications for judicial review of the decisions to detain them.
Each claimant had been detained at Oakington Reception Centre for a period of between 7 to 10 days while their claims were determined. Their claims having been refused, they were then released on temporary admission pending appeal. They had complained that since there had been no risk of their absconding or acting contrary to the public good, their claims could have been determined without detaining them and that accordingly their detention (i) infringed under domestic law an implied requirement that detention under the administrative powers given para 16(1) of Sch 2 to the Immigration Act 1971 had to be necessary; and (ii) infringed art 5(1)(f) of the Convention: "No one shall be deprived of his liberty [unless] ...(f) ... to prevent his effecting an unauthorised entry into the country ...".

LORD SLYNN OF HADLEY said that the argument based on domestic law ignored the reality. Large numbers of applicants had to be considered intensively in a short period. If people failed to arrive on time or at all the programme would be disrupted and delays caused not only to the individual case but to dealing with the whole problem. The need for speed justified detention for a short period in acceptable physical conditions as being reasonably necessary. As for the Convention law argument, detention for the purpose of inquiring whether a claimant was to be granted asylum did fall within art 5(1)(f). There was no provision in that paragraph requiring "necessity" to be shown in order to justify detention. Nor was detention to achieve speedy asylum decision-making a disproportionate response to the reasonable requirements of immigration control. Neither the methods of selection of cases (were they suitable for speedy decision?) nor the objective (speedy decision) nor the way in which people were held for a short period and in reasonable physical conditions even if involving compulsory detention could be said to be arbitrary or disproportionate.
LORD NICHOLLS OF BIRKENHEAD, LORD MUSTILL, LORD HUTTON and LORD SCOTT OF FOSCOTE agreed.
