CRIME — Contraband — Confiscation order — Defendant passing customs posts at river mouth without paying duty on cigarettes — Stopped up river and cigarettes forfeited — Fraudulent evasion of duty — Whether benefiting from offence — Whether deriving "pecuniary advantage" — Criminal Justice Act 1988 (c 33) (as amended by Proceeds of Crime Act 1995 (c 11), s 1(1)(2)), s 71(1A)(4)(5)

R v Smith (David)

HL: Lord Bingham of Cornhill, Lord Nolan, Lord Hoffmann, Lord Hutton and Lord Rodger of Earlsferry: 13 December 2001

The defendant had derived a pecuniary advantage within s 71 of the Criminal Justice Act 1988 when he had passed customs posts at the mouth of a river without paying excise duty on a cargo of cigarettes even though he had been stopped up river by customs officers and the cigarettes had been seized and forfeited.

The House of Lords so held in allowing an appeal by the Crown from the Court of Appeal (Criminal Division) (Mance LJ, Newman and Burton JJ), which on 16 June 2000 had allowed an appeal by the defendant, David Cadman Smith, against a confiscation order made by Judge Moore at Sheffield Crown Court.

LORD BINGHAM OF CORNHILL, LORD NOLAN, LORD HOFFMANN and LORD HUTTON agreed with Lord Rodger of Earlsferry.

LORD RODGER OF EARLSFERRY said that he agreed with Laws LJ in R v Dimsey and Allen [2000] 1 Cr App R(S) 497 when he had said, at p 500, that the ordinary and natural meaning of "pecuniary advantage" included the case where a debt was evaded or deferred. Thus the defendant had derived a pecuniary advantage when he had evaded payment of the customs duty at the customs posts at Immingham and Hull, and that remained the case even though the cigarettes had been seized by the customs officers when they had boarded the defendant's boat at Goole. Under s 71(4) of the 1968 Act the court was concerned simply with the value of the property to the offender "when he obtained it" (s 74(5)(a)); it made no difference if, after he had obtained it, it was destroyed or damaged or forfeited by customs officers. The defendant had, accordingly, benefited from the offence under s 71 and the order of the Crown Court should be restored.

Appearances: Andrew Mitchell QC and Kennedy Talbot (Solicitor, HM Customs and Excise) for the Crown; Ben Emmerson QC and Peter Weatherby (Howells, Sheffield) for the defendant.

Reported by: Michael Gardner, barrister
