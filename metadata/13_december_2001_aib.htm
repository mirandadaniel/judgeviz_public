MORTGAGE — Business partners mortgaging jointly owned property - Covenant that partners jointly and severally liable for all money loaned to them by bank — Whether one partner liable for other partner's personal indebtedness to bank

AIB Group (UK) Ltd v Martin and Another

HL: Lord Irvine of Lairg LC, Lord Hutton, Lord Millett, Lord Scott of Foscote and Lord Rodger of Earlsferry: 13 December 2001

Under the terms of a joint mortgage taken out by two business partners which provided that: "If the expression ‘the mortgagor' includes more than one person it shall be construed as referring to all and/or any one of those persons and the obligations of such persons hereunder shall be joint and several" and "The mortgagor hereby covenants with … the bank… that it will on demand pay or discharge to the bank … all sums of money … advanced to the mortgagor by the bank…". one partner was liable to the bank for the personal indebtedness, unconnected with the partnership, incurred by the other partner.

The House of Lords so held in dismissing an appeal by the second defendant, Mr Alan Clive Gold from a decision of the Court of Appeal (Morritt and Sedley LJJ and Sir Christopher Slade) given on 27 June 2000 dismissing his appeal from a decision of Jacob J given on 15 March 1999 by which he found that the second defendant was liable to the plaintiff bank, AIB Group (UK) Plc (formerly Allied Irish Banks Plc and AIB Finance Ltd) for debts incurred by the first defendant, Mr Martin.

LORD SCOTT OF FOSCOTE said that it had been contended for the second defendant that because "the mortgagor", as defined, included more than one person it should be construed as referring first to the two of them, next to one of them, and then to the other of them. If that were right, the second defendant would not be liable to repay sums advanced to the first defendant alone. However, the relevant clause started with a joint covenant by the defendants. It was not three separate covenants, one by them jointly and one by each of them individually. It was a single joint covenant. Their liability under that joint covenant was declared to be joint and several. That dealt with the effect of their joint covenant. It did not turn a single covenant into three covenants. The critical issue was not whether the defendants, as well as jointly covenanting to pay, had severally covenanted to pay. The critical issue is what had they covenanted to pay? Under the clause they had covenanted to pay "all sums of money ... advanced to the mortgagor by the bank ... ". The mortgagor meant the two of them and/or each of them. So they had covenanted to pay all sums of money advanced by the bank to the two of them and/or to each of them. They had covenanted to pay the sums advanced by the bank to first defendant alone as well as the sums advanced by the bank to them jointly. The clause constituted a covenant by the first defendant and the second defendant to pay their joint debts to the bank, to pay the first defendant's debts to the bank and to pay the second defendant's debts to the bank.

LORD IRVINE OF LAIRG LC, LORD HUTTON and LORD RODGER OF EARLSFERRY delivered concurring judgments. LORD MILLETT delivered a judgment concurring in the result.

Appearances: Nicholas Davidson QC and Howard Smith (Beveridge Milton) for the second defendant; Michael Briggs QC, Jeremy Cousins QC and John Brennan (Moran & Co, Tamworth) for the bank.
