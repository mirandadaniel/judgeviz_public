C R Smith Glaziers (Dunfermline) Ltd v Commissioners of Customs and Excise

Revenue — Value added tax — Exemptions — Insurance services supplied in connection with supply of goods and services — Customer to be provided with "a statement setting out every amount" allocated to insurance services — Whether precise monetary figure required

C R Smith Glaziers (Dunfermline) Ltd v Commissioners of Customs and Excise

HL: Lord Hoffmann, Lord Slynn of Hadley, Lord Woolf, Lord Hope of Craighead and Lord Walker of Gestingthorpe: 20 February 2003

The requirement that "a statement setting out every amount" of the cost of insurance services supplied in association with a supply of goods be provided to the customer pursuant to Note (5) of Group 2 of Sch 9 to the Value Added Tax Act 1994, as substituted by s 38 of the Finance Act 1997, thus enabling the insurance cost to be exempt from VAT, was satisfied when a formula for the calculation of such cost was provided. The supplier was not required to provide the precise monetary figure.
The House of Lords so held (Lord Slynn of Hadley dissenting) in allowing an appeal by the taxpayer, C R Smith Glaziers (Dunfermline) Ltd, against a decision of the Inner House of the Court of Session (Lord Kirkwood, Lord Cameron of Lochbroom and Lord Abernethy) of 16 February 2001 (2001 SC 646) dismissing the taxpayer's appeal from a decision of the Edinburgh VAT and Duties Tribunal (J Gordon Reid QC, RLH Crawford and JD Crerar) dated 22 October 1999 concerning an assessment to VAT by the Commissioners of Customs and Excise.

LORD HOFFMANN said that the taxpayer supplied double glazing. It gave a 10 year guarantee supported by an insurance policy to protect the customer against the risk of its insolvency. The charge to the customer consisted of the price of the double glazing and additional charges for the insurance premium and services provided in arranging and administering the policy. The supply of those additional services was prima facie exempt from VAT. The question was whether the contractual document contained the "statements" required by Note (5). There was no dispute that it stated the amount of the premium, which was specified as £16. But the Commissioners said that it did not contain a statement "setting out every amount that the customer is…required to pay otherwise than by way of such premium". A reference to "10%" of the "total contract price" was in their submission inadequate. Looking at the matter as an ordinary question of construction, which required one to consider the language against the background of the whole statutory scheme, the arguments were finely balanced. What decisively tilted the balance against the Commissioners' construction was the European dimension. The exemption of insurance-related services from VAT was a mandatory requirement of art 13B(a) of the Sixth Council Directive on turnover taxes (77/388/EEC) of 17 May 1977. The only way in which, conformably with the Directive, the United Kingdom could justify rules which deprived such services of exemption was by reliance on the provision that exemption should be under conditions which member states had laid down "for the purpose of ensuring the correct and straightforward application of the exemptions and of preventing any possible evasion, avoidance or abuse". However, there was great difficulty in seeing how a provision insisting not merely that the documents disclosed the apportionment of consideration but did so in a particular form could be said to be "suitable for the purpose" of preventing avoidance. Any additional formal requirement, non-compliance with which might result in denial of exemption, was an additional burden. A requirement that information be communicated in any particular form was more burdensome than a requirement that it might be communicated in any form. And where, as in the instant case, the addition of a requirement of form appeared to serve no legitimate purpose, it was also disproportionate. The Commissioners' construction did not conform to the terms of the Sixth Directive and it was therefore necessary to adopt the alternative construction which did.

LORD HOPE OF CRAIGHEAD delivered a concurring opinion. LORD WOOLF and LORD WALKER OF GESTINGTHORPE agreed. LORD SLYNN OF HADLEY delivered a dissenting opinion.
